Liquid milk Humans have used milk since the beginning of recorded history to provide both fresh and storable nutritious foods. Fermented milk: Other milk products are frequently produced using fermented milk. They are produced by fermenting milk with the right microbes to get the correct level of acidity. Yogurt, koumiss, Dahi, labneh, ergo, tara, kurut, and kefir are examples of fermented foods. Cheeses: are created by the casein milk protein (which is isolated from the whey of the milk) coagulating. There are hundreds of different types of cheese manufactured, many of which are regionally specific. However, wealthy nations are where the majority of cheese is made. Cheese comes in a variety of hardnesses, including unripened, hard, and semi-hard. The variety of cheese's qualities results from variations in the types and compositions of milk, the techniques utilized, and the microorganisms utilized. Ayub, Gibna Bayda, Chanco, Queso Fresco, Akawieh, and Chhurpi are traditional cheeses made in developing nations.

Butter and ghee: The process of making butter involves churning milk or cream; in many underdeveloped nations, traditional butter is made by churning sour whole milk. Butter is made into ghee by draining the water out of it, and it is especially well-liked in South Asia. Ghee can be kept fresh for up to two years. Dry milk or milk powder is produced by drying milk, and it typically takes the shape of powder or granules. Cream: is removed from milk by skimming or centrifuging, and it is the portion that is relatively high in milk fat. Products made from cream include whipped cream, fermented cream, acidified cream, prepared creams, pre-packaged liquid cream, recombined cream, and reconstituted cream.
